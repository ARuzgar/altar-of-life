# The Premise

> *You are already dead. The question is what you do about it.*

## The Core Concept

The players of Altar of Life are not adventurers. They are not heroes. They are **the dead** — souls who have arrived at a threshold between oblivion and return, drawn to the Altar by something unresolved: a burning desire, an unfinished story, a refusal to simply cease.

The Altar does not grant resurrection freely. It is not benevolent. It is a mechanism — ancient, indifferent, and exacting. It offers a bargain: **compete, sacrifice, outlast**, and perhaps you will be allowed to return. Perhaps.

The dead play against each other not out of hatred but out of need. There is only one path back. There is only one winner.

## The Altar of Life

The Altar is the center of the world — the game board, the economy, the judge. What it is exactly is still being defined, but it functions as:

- The place where bids are made and health is offered
- The source from which cards (Souls, Rites, Relics) are drawn and presented
- The entity (or system) that sets the rules of the game

**Open lore question:** Is the Altar *sentient*? Is it run by a figure (a death god, an arbiter, a ferryman)? Or is it an ancient machine, grinding on without intention? This changes the tone significantly — a sentient Altar creates narrative; a mechanical one creates dread.

*See [[Inspirations]] — Death Parade's Decim, Sandman's Death, and Charon are all models for different versions of this figure.*

## The Players — Who Are the Dead?

Each player represents a soul with a reason to return. This could be:

- A lover who died before reconciling
- A warrior who died with honor unfulfilled
- A parent who left children behind
- A scholar who died before completing something world-changing
- Someone who simply wasn't ready

**In mechanical terms**, this is where character identity and potentially starting deck composition or bonus abilities could come from. Soul backgrounds as an optional setup module.

**In tonal terms**, this is where the melodramatic-romantic register lives. These are not abstract tokens — they are people who *feel* too much to stay dead.

## The Health — What Is It, Really?

In lore, health is not hit points. It is **soul energy** — the remnant vitality a dead person carries into the threshold. It is the echo of having been alive.

When you bid health to acquire power, you are literally spending pieces of yourself. When you reach zero, you are not "defeated" — you are *extinguished*. The last echo fades. There is no coming back from that.

This makes the Altar's bargain doubly cruel: to win resurrection, you must risk permanent annihilation.

## The Cards — What Are They?

In lore, the cards that appear at the Altar are not arbitrary:

- **Souls** are the spirits of others who have passed through this place before — echoes, memories, entities that can be called into service. To summon a Soul is to make a deal with another dead.
- **Rites** (or **Calls**, TBD) are the old words — prayers, curses, invocations that still carry power in this place between worlds.
- **Relics** are objects: things the dead carried with them, or things left behind by those who came before. A charm. A weapon. A letter never sent. Objects with weight.

## Tone

This is a game about **beautiful desperation**. The mood is:
- The last night before something inevitable
- A love letter written knowing it will never be read
- Defiance in the face of oblivion — not heroic defiance, but *human* defiance

Think Persona 3's final moments. Think the contestants in Death Parade who wept not from fear but from remembering. Think every folk song about someone bargaining with death.

## Open Questions

- What is the Altar's nature — sentient arbiter, ancient machine, or something else?
- Does the winner actually get resurrected, or is resurrection a lie the Altar tells? (A darker reading: maybe this is Purgatory and "winning" just means something different)
- Are there NPCs in this space? Souls who have been here too long and no longer want to return?
- Does the video game adaptation expand the lore with a narrative layer, while the tabletop focuses on the game?
